Your RAG system is only as good as your retrieval step, and your retrieval step is only as good as your embeddings. Understanding how embeddings work — and why vector search sometimes fails — is what separates a RAG system that works from one that works in demos.

6.1 What Embeddings Actually Are

An embedding is a list of numbers (a vector) that encodes the semantic meaning of a piece of text. Two pieces of text with similar meaning produce vectors that are close together in this high-dimensional space — regardless of whether they share the same words.

Think of embeddings as GPS coordinates for meaning. "The dog chased the ball" and "A canine pursued a sphere" are at almost the same coordinates, even though they share zero words. Keyword search would miss this; embedding-based search catches it. 
# Example: embeddings are just arrays of floats
from voyageai import Client as VoyageClient  # or openai, cohere, etc.

voyage = VoyageClient()

texts = [
    "The dog chased the ball",
    "A canine pursued a sphere",
    "The stock market crashed yesterday",
]

result = voyage.embed(texts, model="voyage-3")
embeddings = result.embeddings  # list of 1024-float vectors

# Semantic similarity via dot product (vectors are normalized):
import numpy as np
sim = np.dot(embeddings[0], embeddings[1])
print(f"Dog/canine similarity: {sim:.3f}")   # ~0.95 — very similar
sim2 = np.dot(embeddings[0], embeddings[2])
print(f"Dog/stock similarity:  {sim2:.3f}")  # ~0.12 — very different
6.2 Choosing an Embedding Model

The embedding model you choose affects retrieval quality more than your vector database or search algorithm. Pick one based on your domain, latency requirements, and cost.

ModelDimensionsBest forNotes
voyage-31024General purpose, code, multi-lingualBest quality for RAG
text-embedding-3-large3072OpenAI ecosystemMatryoshka — can truncate dims
text-embedding-3-small1536Cost-sensitive appsGood quality/cost ratio
nomic-embed-text768Local/offline useOpen source, runs on CPU
Never mix embedding models within the same vector store. If you indexed 10,000 documents with model A and search with model B, the similarity scores are meaningless — the vectors live in different semantic spaces. Lock your embedding model and version it.
6.3 Vector Databases: When to Use One

You don't need a dedicated vector database until you have tens of thousands of chunks and low-latency requirements. For small corpora, NumPy cosine similarity or SQLite with pgvector is enough. For production scale, use a purpose-built store.

6.4 Reranking: Fix Retrieval Quality Without Changing Your Index

Vector search retrieves fast but imprecisely. A reranker takes your top-K candidates and re-scores them with a heavier model that actually reads both the query and each chunk side-by-side. This two-stage approach is the standard production pattern.

import voyageai

voyage = voyageai.Client()

# Stage 1: fast vector search → top 20 candidates
candidates = vector_db.search(query, top_k=20)

# Stage 2: rerank → keep top 5
reranked = voyage.rerank(
    query=query,
    documents=[c["text"] for c in candidates],
    model="rerank-2",
    top_k=5
)

# Use reranked results in the prompt
best_chunks = [candidates[r.index] for r in reranked.results]
💡 Adding a reranker to an existing RAG pipeline is the highest ROI improvement you can make. It often takes a mediocre RAG system to production-ready quality in one afternoon of work — no re-indexing, no new vector DB, just an extra API call.
6.5 Verify: Evaluate Your Retrieval Quality

Build a small eval set: 10 questions with known-correct source chunks. Run your retrieval pipeline and measure hit rate (was the correct chunk in the top-5?). A number below 70% means your chunking or embedding needs work — not your prompt.

  • I chose an embedding model and documented why (quality/cost/latency tradeoff)
  • I picked a vector store appropriate for my data volume and set it up

  • I created at least 10 query/expected-source pairs to evaluate my retrieval quality numerically
